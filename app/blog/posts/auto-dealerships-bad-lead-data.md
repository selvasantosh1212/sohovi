# How Auto Dealerships Lose Deals to Bad Lead Data (And What to Do About It)

A dealer spends $500 on a lead from a third-party source. The lead enters the CRM. Two days later, the same person submits through the website. Now there are two records, two salespeople assigned, and the prospect gets two calls in the same afternoon from the same dealership.

The prospect chooses a competitor.

Auto dealerships are among the highest per-lead-cost businesses in existence. Bad lead data directly destroys ROI on that spend.

## Where Auto Dealer Lead Data Goes Wrong

### Duplicate Leads From Multiple Sources

A typical dealer generates leads from: the website, Cars.com, AutoTrader, Carfax, CarGurus, manufacturer portals, phone-in leads, walk-ins, and referrals. Each source creates a record. When the same prospect appears through two sources, both sources create separate CRM records.

Duplicate rates in auto dealer CRMs range from 15–35% in databases without active deduplication. That means up to a third of the leads you're paying per-lead fees on are people already in your database.

### Invalid Contact Information

Lead quality from third-party sources varies significantly. Leads who entered a fake phone number to see a price are immediately uncontactable. Even real phone numbers have voicemail that's full, is never checked, or was transposed.

Contacts entered quickly on mobile have a higher typo rate. An email address that's almost right (gmail vs. gamil, .com vs. .cm) appears valid but bounces.

### CRM Stage and Assignment Problems

A lead that's been contacted and doesn't fit the immediate buying window gets marked as "unqualified" and archived — but is actually a 6-month buyer. When they're ready, the original inquiry is buried and they go back to the market rather than receiving a timely follow-up.

A lead assigned to a salesperson who left the dealership and wasn't reassigned receives no follow-up until someone notices the unassigned records.

### Inconsistent Note Practices

One sales rep logs every call with detailed notes. Another logs only "left voicemail." A third doesn't log calls at all. When a manager reviews the CRM, the data is useless for understanding the actual lead status.

### Missing Purchase Intent Signals

Leads with vehicle of interest fields left blank, without trade-in information captured, or without financial qualification notes can't be properly prioritized or worked effectively. The lead exists; the information to work it effectively doesn't.

## The Dealership Lead Data Audit

### Daily: Duplicate Check on New Leads

Every lead that enters the CRM should be checked for an existing record before it's assigned. Most automotive CRMs (VinSolutions, DealerSocket, Reynolds & Reynolds) have built-in duplicate detection — make sure it's enabled and configured.

For leads entering without automatic deduplication, BDC staff should search before creating a new record. Search by phone AND email — the same prospect often uses different contact info across different sources.

### Weekly: Assignment and Status Review

- Which active leads have no activity in the past 7 days?
- Which leads are assigned to salespeople who are no longer active?
- Which leads have been in the same stage for more than 30 days without a scheduled activity?

These are the leads that fall through cracks. A weekly 30-minute review catches them before they're lost.

### Monthly: Contact Validity Check

- What percentage of recent leads have invalid email addresses (bounced or clearly fake)?
- What percentage have phone numbers that are disconnected or never answered?

High rates on either metric suggest a lead source quality problem — the source is delivering contacts that can't be reached, regardless of what you pay per lead.

## Improving Lead Data at Intake

**Real-time phone verification on web forms**: Services that validate whether a phone number is real and mobile vs. landline at the moment of form submission reduce fake-number leads.

**Email verification at form submission**: Real-time email syntax checking catches obvious typos and format errors. Domain-level verification catches common misspellings.

**Required vehicle of interest field**: Make the vehicle interest field required. A lead without a vehicle of interest is harder to work and harder to route to the right salesperson.

**Source tagging at all entry points**: Every lead, regardless of source, should have the source tagged in the CRM at creation. Without source tagging, you can't measure lead quality by source and can't make informed decisions about where to spend the lead budget.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What duplicate rate should we expect in a dealer CRM?**
Without active deduplication, 15–30%. With enabled CRM deduplication and consistent intake practices, 3–8%. The gap between those numbers represents leads you're paying for twice.

**Q: Should we delete duplicate lead records or merge them?**
Merge, with full history consolidated on the primary record. A customer who appears as a website lead AND a Cars.com lead has shown high interest — that information is valuable. The primary record should reflect both touch points.

**Q: How do we handle leads from third-party aggregators that charge per lead but are often duplicates?**
Document your duplicate rate for each lead source and factor it into your effective cost-per-lead calculation. A source with a 30% duplicate rate is 43% more expensive than the sticker price. Use that data in vendor negotiations.

**Q: What's the ROI impact of fixing lead data quality?**
Highly variable, but even modest improvements compound. Reducing duplicate rate from 25% to 10% means 20% more effective leads from the same spend. Reducing uncontactable rates from 20% to 8% means 15% more leads that can actually be worked.
