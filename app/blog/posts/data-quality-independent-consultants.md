# Data Quality for Independent Consultants: Keeping Client Data Professional

Your client hired you for your expertise. They sent you their revenue data, customer analysis, and operational spreadsheets. They expect you to make sense of it and give them recommendations they can trust.

The accuracy of those recommendations depends entirely on the quality of the data behind them. And the data they sent you? It probably has problems you haven't found yet.

## Why Data Quality Is a Consulting Problem, Not Just a Technical One

Consultants who work with data — whether in strategy, operations, marketing, finance, or any other function — are making recommendations based on that data. If the data is wrong, the recommendations are wrong. If the data is incomplete, the recommendations have gaps. If the data is inconsistent, the analysis contradicts itself.

Data quality isn't a technical concern that someone else handles. It's the foundation of the analytical work that justifies your fees.

The consultants who get called back are the ones whose recommendations held up. The ones who don't get called back often delivered confident recommendations built on data that turned out to be unreliable.

## The Risk Profile of Consulting Data

Consultants typically receive data in several forms, each with characteristic quality risks:

**Client-exported spreadsheets**: Often contain unfiltered records, legacy data from old systems, inconsistent formatting from multiple contributors, and no documentation of what columns mean. The risk is misinterpretation — using a column in the wrong way because you didn't know its actual meaning.

**CRM reports and exports**: Frequently contain duplicates (the same customer in multiple pipeline stages), incomplete data (fields that weren't used consistently), and stale records (contacts that left the company, deals that closed long ago). The risk is inaccurate aggregate analysis — counts and averages that include records they shouldn't.

**Financial data**: Often has duplicate transactions (bank imports plus manual entries), miscategorized items, and period errors (items in the wrong fiscal period). The risk is wrong numbers in recommendations that carry financial weight.

**Survey and interview data**: May have inconsistent response coding, response bias that's undocumented, and quality issues that affect generalizability. The risk is drawing broader conclusions than the data supports.

## The Data Quality Checks Every Consultant Should Run

Before building any analysis on client data, run these checks:

### Check 1: Understand What You Have

Don't assume columns mean what their names imply. Ask for documentation, or at minimum, ask your client contact: "Can you walk me through what each column in this file means and where the data comes from?"

Especially verify:
- Columns with ambiguous names
- Columns where the values don't match what you'd expect
- Any column you might use as a filter or segmentation criterion

### Check 2: Completeness on Your Key Variables

For the variables central to your analysis, check completion rates. If your analysis depends on the "industry" field and 40% of records don't have it, your industry segmentation is based on 60% of the data — a fact that must be disclosed in your deliverable.

### Check 3: Duplicates in Entities You're Counting

If you're recommending strategies based on customer count, segment size, or pipeline value, duplicates in those records directly distort your recommendations. A customer count that includes 15% duplicates is an overstated baseline.

### Check 4: Consistency in Variables You'll Compare

If you're comparing sales across regions, or performance across customer segments, the definitions and data in those categorical fields must be consistent. Inconsistent data in the comparison variable produces an apples-to-oranges analysis.

### Check 5: Outlier Review in Numeric Fields

Sort your key numeric fields and look at both extremes. A $50M revenue figure in a dataset where all other entries are in the $10K–$500K range is either correct (and needs special handling) or a data entry error (and needs to be corrected). Outliers that remain in your analysis will distort averages, regressions, and benchmarks.

## How to Handle Data Quality Issues in Client Deliverables

When you find quality problems in client data, you have three options:

**Fix and note**: For problems with clear, unambiguous corrections (duplicate records, format standardization), fix them and note what you changed and why in your methodology section.

**Exclude and note**: For records that are incomplete or invalid for your analysis, exclude them and document the exclusion criteria and count in your deliverable. "This analysis includes 4,200 of the 5,000 records provided. 800 records were excluded because the industry classification was missing."

**Flag and caveat**: For problems that you can't resolve independently — data whose meaning is ambiguous, quality issues that might be significant but require client input — flag them in a limitations section and acknowledge the uncertainty in your conclusions.

The worst option: ignore quality problems and deliver conclusions without disclosing the limitations. This is how consulting relationships break down when clients discover the analysis was built on flawed assumptions.

## Building a Consulting Data Quality Standard

Consistent quality standards across your engagements mean:

1. Every deliverable has a methodology section that describes the data you used, any quality issues you found, and how you handled them
2. You always disclose your analysis population (how many records, what filters applied)
3. You run the same basic quality checks at the start of every analytical engagement
4. You use Sohovi or similar tools to quickly profile any CSV before building analysis on it

Clients who see this level of rigor — even when the findings are "the data was fine" — trust your work more than clients who receive analysis without methodology documentation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What should I do if the client says the data quality problems are not my problem?**
Document that the client has been notified of the issues and has directed you to proceed without addressing them. Include appropriate caveats in your deliverable. You've fulfilled your professional responsibility; the client has made an informed decision.

**Q: Is it worth charging for data quality investigation time?**
Yes. Discovering, documenting, and resolving data quality issues is professional work with real value. Include it in your project scope as "data review and quality assessment" — don't absorb it into project time without acknowledgment.

**Q: How do I communicate data limitations without undermining my recommendations?**
Frame limitations as context, not disclaimers: "Based on the 4,200 complete records representing approximately 84% of the total dataset, our analysis indicates..." This is more professional than burying "n=4,200 of 5,000" in a footnote that clients won't read.

**Q: What's the minimum data quality check a busy consultant can run?**
Completeness on your 3 key variables and a duplicate check on the primary entity you're analyzing. That's 15 minutes in a spreadsheet and catches the problems most likely to distort your outputs.

**The quality of your analysis is only as good as the quality of the data behind it. Taking 30 minutes at the start of an analytical engagement to understand and document the data you're working with isn't overhead — it's the professional standard that protects both your recommendations and your reputation.**
