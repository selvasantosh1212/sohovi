# Data Quality for PR Agencies: Keeping Media Contact Lists Accurate and Current

Sending a press release about a fintech product to a journalist who now covers beauty? Reaching out to an editor who left the publication six months ago? Emailing a media contact at an address that bounced?

Each of these is a data quality failure that costs credibility with editors, wastes pitch opportunities, and damages the agency's professional reputation. Media contact list quality is the unglamorous but mission-critical foundation of PR outreach.

## Why Media Contact Lists Decay So Fast

Journalist databases are among the fastest-decaying data in marketing. The journalism industry has high turnover — reporters change beats, change publications, go freelance, or leave the industry entirely. LinkedIn estimates that media jobs have among the highest turnover rates in any professional sector.

Specific decay patterns:
- A reporter covers tech startups → promoted to senior editor of a different section → still on your tech startup list
- A journalist leaves a publication → joins a competitor → your outreach goes to their old address (which may not even bounce, just go to a shared inbox no one checks)
- A freelancer covers your client's space occasionally → you add them to a regular list → they land 60% of your pitches in spam because they've never opted in
- A contact database purchased from a media intelligence service → 18 months later, 20–30% of the contacts have moved on

## The Media Contact List Quality Checklist

### After Every Major Outreach Campaign: Bounce Review

Check the bounce rate on your last pitch campaign. Addresses that hard-bounced are definitively wrong — remove them from your list and research the journalist's current contact.

A media list that produces more than 5% hard bounces on a pitch send has significant staleness and needs a systematic refresh.

### Before Each Campaign: Beat Verification Sample Check

For every major pitch campaign, spot-check 20–30% of the contacts against their current published bios or social profiles:
- Are they still at the same publication?
- Are they still covering the beat relevant to this pitch?
- Is the contact email still the one on record?

A 10-minute spot check catches the most obvious mismatches before you pitch.

### Quarterly: Full List Refresh

Quarterly, run through the full list with a systematic verification:
- Cross-reference each contact against their publication's staff page or bio
- Check LinkedIn for any job changes in the past 90 days
- Remove anyone who has left journalism or moved to a clearly irrelevant role
- Add updated contact information for journalists you've found have moved

This is the investment that keeps your list usable. A list that was excellent 18 months ago and hasn't been refreshed is now unreliable.

## Adding "Last Verified" Date to Every Record

The single most useful metadata addition to a media contact list: a "last verified" date for each record. This field answers "when did someone last confirm this contact is still accurate?"

Sort your list by last verified date. Any contact verified more than 6 months ago should be flagged for reverification before use in a major campaign.

Without this field, every record looks equally current — even ones that haven't been verified in two years.

## Building a Tiered Contact List

Not all media contacts are equal in terms of maintenance priority:

**Tier 1 (verify monthly)**: Your 20–30 most important contacts — the reporters who cover your clients most frequently, who you pitch most often, or who have the highest impact coverage.

**Tier 2 (verify quarterly)**: Regular contacts you pitch 2–4 times per year.

**Tier 3 (verify before use)**: Contacts you use occasionally — check each one before including in a specific campaign, not on a regular schedule.

This tiered approach directs your verification effort to where it has the most impact.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should PR agencies use media database tools (Cision, Muck Rack, Prowly) or maintain their own lists?**
Both. Media databases provide broad coverage and automated updates but can have their own accuracy issues — especially for niche beats or smaller publications. Supplement with your own curated lists for your most important and most-used contacts.

**Q: What do I do when a key media contact goes unresponsive?**
Check whether they're still at the publication and still on the same beat. If yes, try a different contact method (some journalists prefer social direct message over email for pitches). If their information has changed, update the record.

**Q: How do I handle pitching journalists who have gone freelance?**
Freelancers are valuable contacts — they may write for multiple outlets relevant to your clients. Update their record to reflect freelance status, add their personal professional email (not a publication email), and note which outlets they regularly write for.

**Q: Is it worth paying for automated contact verification services?**
For large agency lists (1,000+ contacts), yes. Services that automatically flag bounces, monitor for changes, and alert when contacts move can significantly reduce manual verification work. Evaluate the cost against the time your team spends on manual verification.

**A media contact list that's current and accurate is a competitive advantage for a PR agency. Every journalist you reach with a relevant, correctly targeted pitch is one your competitors — working from a less-maintained list — might be missing. Invest in list maintenance as a core operational activity, not an afterthought before campaigns.**
