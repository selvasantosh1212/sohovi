# Data Quality for Veterinary Clinics: Keeping Pet and Owner Records Accurate

A dog comes in for an annual wellness visit. The record in your practice management system still lists the owner who surrendered the dog to a rescue. The rescue adopted the dog out a year ago, and the new owner's contact information was added as a note rather than properly entered. When the next annual reminder goes out, it reaches nobody.

Veterinary practices depend on accurate, complete records to deliver preventive care, maintain client relationships, and run effective recall and reminder workflows. Pet healthcare is almost entirely relationship-driven — a client who feels recognized and well-served stays.

## The Specific Data Quality Challenges in Veterinary Practice

### Multi-Pet, Multi-Owner Records

A household may have three pets under different owner names — the pets were adopted at different times, and different family members registered each one. Three separate client records, same household.

Combined-household records make communication more targeted and billing more efficient, but they require accurate record linking.

### Pet Status Inaccuracies

A patient record that still shows a pet as "active" when they've passed is a painful mistake — sending a vaccine reminder to a client whose pet has died. This is both clinically meaningless and an emotionally harmful client experience.

Pet status updates (deceased, transferred to another clinic, new owner) must be captured in real time.

### Outdated Vaccination Records

Vaccination dates that haven't been updated after visits. Vaccines marked as due that were already given at a previous visit. Records that don't reflect vaccines given elsewhere.

Inaccurate vaccination records undermine both clinical safety (risk of over-vaccination or under-vaccination) and client trust when the records are reviewed in the exam room.

### Duplicate Client/Patient Records

The same pet under two different owner records. Two records for the same pet (different staff members created a new record instead of finding the existing one). Merged families where the husband's and wife's records both contain some of the pets.

Duplicate records split medical history — a clinician reviewing one record misses treatments and findings documented in the other.

### Outdated Contact Information

The phone number that was valid two years ago. The email from a job the client left. The address before they moved. Reminder calls that hit disconnected numbers, appointment confirmation emails that bounce, postcard reminders returned as undeliverable.

## The Veterinary Practice Data Quality Checklist

### At Every Check-In

- Verbally confirm: "Is your address still [address]? Phone still [number]? Same email for appointment reminders?"
- If a pet's status has changed (new pet in the household, a pet has passed), update immediately
- Confirm emergency contact is current for boarding or surgery patients

This 30-second intake step catches the vast majority of stale information before it causes a problem.

### Monthly: Reminder Failure Review

- Check your last month's reminder sends for bounce and failure rates
- Flag client records where reminders haven't delivered successfully
- Update contact information for flagged records on a rolling basis

### Quarterly: Pet Status Audit

- Export active patient list
- Sort by last visit date
- Flag patients with no visit in 18+ months for a status review
- Patients who have moved to a different clinic should be marked "Transferred" and removed from active recall

### Annually: Vaccination Record Review

- Spot check vaccination records for active patients
- Verify that vaccines given at your clinic are correctly recorded with dates
- Check for vaccination due dates that are clearly wrong (past the theoretical maximum, or scheduled before the previous vaccine in a series)

## Building the Intake and Exit Habits

**At intake**: Always search for the existing client record before creating a new one. Search by owner last name AND pet name to catch records entered under variations.

**At exit**: Update the chart before the client leaves. Vaccine records, weight, next appointment, any new diagnoses or medications. Records completed at exit are more accurate than records completed from memory later.

**After a loss**: When a client informs you a pet has passed, update the record immediately with status "Deceased" and the date. Set a reminder to send a condolence communication if appropriate. This moment is important to the client — handling it with care builds long-term loyalty.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I handle records for pets with multiple owners (divorce, shared custody)?**
Create a primary owner record with all pets linked. Add the secondary owner as a co-owner with contact information noted. Billing typically goes to the primary; reminders go to the contact on file. Clarify with the owners who receives which communications.

**Q: What's the right interval for a full patient record audit?**
Annual for a comprehensive review. Quarterly for a targeted contact information and pet status check. The ongoing intake habits (verify at every visit) reduce the scope of formal audits.

**Q: How do I handle data for patients adopted from rescues?**
The rescue may have provided a record. Verify the accuracy of all information at the first visit with the new owner. Update all contact information to the new owner's details, and note the rescue record for vaccination history reference.

**In veterinary practice, the record IS the relationship. A record that's current, complete, and accurate enables preventive care reminders that arrive on time, billing that's correct, and clinical decisions that reflect the full patient history. Build the verification habits into every visit, and the records will stay reliable.**
