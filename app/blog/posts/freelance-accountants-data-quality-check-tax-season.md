# Why Freelance Accountants Need a Data Quality Check Before Tax Season

The tax filing you submit is only as accurate as the data behind it. A $10,000 expense miscategorized as income. A duplicate invoice counted twice. A payment attributed to the wrong vendor. These aren't hypothetical — they're the kind of errors that appear in poorly maintained client books, and they're your professional exposure if they make it into a filing.

A data quality check before tax season catches these problems when they're fixable — not after the filing, when they trigger amendments, IRS notices, or worse.

## What "Data Quality" Means for Financial Records

In accounting, data quality has a specific meaning: every transaction is recorded accurately, once, in the right category, attributed to the right entity, with the correct date and amount.

Failures look like:

**Duplicate transactions**: The same invoice paid twice in the records. Often happens when a payment is entered manually and also imported from the bank feed. The duplication inflates expenses or income and produces an incorrect reconciled balance.

**Miscategorized expenses**: Office supplies recorded as equipment (affecting depreciation calculations). Marketing expenses recorded as COGS. The filing is complete but the categories are wrong, potentially producing incorrect deductions.

**Missing transactions**: Payments received or made that don't appear in the books. This can be bank transactions that weren't reconciled, cash transactions that weren't recorded, or invoices that were issued but not entered.

**Wrong amounts**: Typos in transaction amounts. A $1,500 invoice entered as $15,000 or $150.

**Wrong dates**: Transactions recorded in the wrong fiscal period. An expense incurred in December but entered in January shifts it to the next tax year.

**Vendor or customer misattribution**: A payment from Customer A recorded against Customer B. A vendor invoice attributed to the wrong supplier, creating a receivables/payables discrepancy.

## The Pre-Tax Season Data Quality Checklist

Run these checks on every client's books before beginning the tax preparation:

### 1. Reconcile All Accounts

Bank accounts, credit card accounts, and loan accounts should all be fully reconciled through the last day of the tax year. Unreconciled transactions are the primary source of missing or duplicate entries.

If an account isn't reconciled, the transaction data it contains is suspect. Reconcile before auditing — reconciliation is the first filter.

### 2. Check for Duplicate Transactions

Export transactions to a spreadsheet and check for duplicates on amount + date + description. In Excel:
- Sort by amount, then by date
- Use `COUNTIFS()` to flag rows where the same amount appears on the same date with the same payee

Not every match is a duplicate — two identical transactions on the same day can be legitimate. But each flagged pair deserves a review.

### 3. Review Unusual Amounts

Sort income and expense transactions by amount. Look at the top and bottom of each range:
- Extremely large transactions for the category (a $50,000 entry in office supplies)
- Negative amounts in income or positive amounts in expense accounts
- Round numbers that are suspiciously even (transactions often aren't exactly $5,000.00)
- Amounts that end in .00 when the vendor typically invoices with cents

### 4. Verify Transaction Dates Fall in the Right Period

For accrual-basis filers, transactions must be recorded in the period when income was earned or expenses were incurred — not when cash changed hands. Check for transactions dated in the filing period that should have been in a prior period.

For cash-basis filers, verify that cash receipt and payment dates are correct.

### 5. Audit Category Assignments

Generate a transaction list sorted by category. Within each category, review whether all transactions actually belong there. A list of transactions in "Meals & Entertainment" that includes two hotel charges and a flight indicates categorization errors.

### 6. Check the Vendor and Customer Lists for Duplicates

Duplicate vendor records produce duplicate payment history — making it look like you've paid a vendor twice when you've only paid once. A vendor appearing as "Acme Corp" and "Acme Corporation" and "Acme Inc" are three records that may be one vendor.

Run a quick check on your vendor and customer lists for obvious name variants of the same entity.

### 7. Verify the Opening Balances

If this is a continued engagement, the prior year ending balances should match the current year opening balances. Any discrepancy indicates something was changed after the prior year close.

## How to Document What You Find

Every finding should be documented before you fix it:

- What the error is
- Which account/transaction it appears in
- What the current value is
- What it should be
- Whether client confirmation is needed

Some errors you can fix independently (obvious typos, clear miscategorizations based on the invoice description). Others need client input (a transaction you can't classify without knowing what it was for). Document the distinction.

## Client Communication About Data Quality Issues

Some clients don't know their books have problems. Finding significant errors creates a sensitive conversation:

- **Frame it constructively**: "I found some transactions that need to be reviewed before I can complete the filing accurately" — not "your bookkeeping is a mess."
- **Be specific**: "There appear to be 12 duplicate expense entries totaling approximately $3,400" is actionable. "The data has issues" is not.
- **Quantify the impact**: "These duplicates would overstate expenses by approximately $3,400, reducing your taxable income by that amount — which would produce an inaccurate filing."
- **Give the client a decision to make**: "Do you want me to remove the duplicates after verification, or would you like to review each one first?"

A client who understands that you caught a problem before it became a filing error — rather than after — will value that thoroughness.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much time should a pre-tax season data audit take?**
For well-maintained books with a full reconciliation, 1–2 hours. For books that haven't been fully reconciled or have known problems, 4–8 hours minimum. The time investment is worth it — every hour spent on the audit prevents multiple hours of amendment work.

**Q: What if the client hasn't kept their books up to date?**
Before tax preparation, the books need to be complete. Either the client needs to catch up, or you need to scope the catch-up bookkeeping as a separate engagement before the tax work begins. Filing a return based on incomplete records exposes both you and the client.

**Q: Should I charge separately for the data quality audit?**
For engagements where books are handed over by the client (vs. you maintaining them throughout the year), yes. The audit is professional work with value that the client should pay for. Frame it as "pre-filing review" and include it in your scope.

**Q: What records should I keep from the audit?**
Document your findings, the changes made, and the client's sign-off on material corrections. This is your engagement work product and your protection if there's ever a question about the accuracy of the filing.

The same problems found in a pre-season audit are resolved with a conversation and a few correcting entries. Run the audit, document what you find, and fix it before the filing. Your clients hired you for exactly this.**
