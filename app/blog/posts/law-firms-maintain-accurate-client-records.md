# How Law Firms Can Maintain Accurate Client Records Without a DBA

Law firms have a professional and ethical obligation to maintain accurate client records — not just because it's good practice, but because client data errors can create conflicts of interest, billing disputes, malpractice exposure, and bar discipline issues.

Small and mid-size firms typically don't have a dedicated database administrator. Records are managed in a practice management system (Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther), a CRM, or sometimes a combination of systems. Here's how to maintain data quality at the practice level.

## Why Client Record Quality Matters for Law Firms

### Conflict of Interest Checking

Before taking a new client matter, firms must run a conflict check — verifying that the firm hasn't previously represented (or adversely represented) any party in a related matter. A conflict check is only as reliable as the data it searches.

If past clients are entered under multiple name variants, if matter parties are inconsistently recorded, or if the records of adverse parties are incomplete, conflicts may go undetected. An undetected conflict is a serious professional responsibility failure.

**The data quality requirement for conflicts**: Every person and entity involved in every matter must be recorded accurately and completely. Nicknames, alternate spellings, maiden names, AKAs, and entity variants (ABC Corp vs. ABC Corporation vs. ABC Co.) all need to be captured.

### Billing Accuracy

Client billing records depend on accurate matter codes, correct billing rates, and accurate time entries. Data quality failures in billing produce:
- Overbilling (a client dispute and ethics issue)
- Underbilling (a revenue problem)
- Incorrect rate application (applying a partner rate to an associate entry)
- Invoice entries for the wrong matter

### Statute of Limitations and Deadline Tracking

Case management relies on accurate dates — filing deadlines, statute of limitations, hearing dates, response due dates. A wrong date in the system or a date not entered creates a malpractice risk. Data completeness in deadline fields is literally a risk management issue.

### Client Communication Records

Correspondence with clients should be accurately associated with the correct client and matter. Misfiled communications create both internal confusion and potential confidentiality issues if the wrong client can access records intended for another.

## The Law Firm Data Quality Checklist

### Conflict Check Database Integrity

Quarterly, audit the conflict database:
- Are all matter parties recorded (plaintiff, defendant, adverse parties, affiliated entities)?
- Are entity names recorded with variants (Inc., LLC., Corp.)?
- Are personal names recorded with known variants (maiden names, AKAs, common name spellings)?

Add missing party information to open and recently closed matters. A conflict database that's incomplete for recent matters is most likely to fail for current conflicts.

### Client Contact Information Currency

Annually, verify contact information for active clients:
- Is the current address correct?
- Is the email address deliverable?
- Is the phone number active?

For inactive clients (matters closed more than 1 year ago), contact information accuracy matters less for active communication but still matters for billing disputes, file transfers, or the client returning.

### Billing Record Completeness

Monthly, before billing:
- Are all time entries for the billing period associated with the correct matter?
- Are billing rates applied correctly to each timekeeper?
- Are any entries marked "no charge" that should be billed?
- Are there any open time entries that haven't been finalized?

A billing quality check before invoice generation catches errors before the client sees them — far better than after.

### Deadline Field Completeness

For open matters, verify that:
- Statute of limitations date is entered (if applicable)
- Next hearing or response date is entered
- Client-reported deadlines are entered

Any open matter with a missing deadline field should be flagged for immediate review.

## Handling Data Quality in Practice Management Software

Most practice management systems have built-in data quality features that many firms underutilize:

**Clio**: Has a conflict search function and required field enforcement. Use custom fields to capture conflict-relevant information beyond default fields.

**MyCase**: Supports required fields and has a conflict check system. Configure it to require that all matter participants are entered.

**PracticePanther**: Similar conflict check and required field functionality.

For firms not using a dedicated practice management system (still using general CRM or spreadsheets): the data quality risk is higher because these tools don't have legal-specific conflict check or deadline tracking features built in. Consider migrating to legal-specific software.

## Reducing Data Entry Errors at the Matter Opening Stage

Matter opening is when most data quality failures originate. The intake team is often processing new clients quickly and may skip optional fields or make entry errors.

**Intake checklist**: Create a matter opening checklist that must be completed before a matter is marked "open." Required fields should include all conflict-relevant parties, all relevant dates, and billing setup.

**Two-person review for conflict checks**: The attorney running the conflict check should verify that all parties and variants were entered before accepting the new matter. This is both a professional responsibility requirement and a data quality check.

**Standardized entity name format**: Define how the firm records entity names (do you write out "Incorporated" or use "Inc."?). Consistent format makes conflict searches more reliable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should small law firms run conflict database audits?**
At minimum annually for a full audit; quarterly for a check of recently opened matters. Running the audit before any large marketing push or referral campaign that might generate new clients is also good practice.

**Q: What should happen when a data quality error is found in a client record?**
Correct it, document what was corrected and when, and evaluate whether the error created any professional responsibility issue. If an incorrect party entry missed a conflict, the matter and the error need to be reviewed for disclosure obligations.

**Q: Can law firms use general data quality tools for client records?**
For demographic and contact data quality checks (completeness, format validation, duplicate detection), general tools work. For conflict database management, use legal-specific software with conflict check functionality — the requirements are too specialized for general tools.
