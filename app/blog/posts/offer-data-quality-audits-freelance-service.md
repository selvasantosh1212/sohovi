# How to Offer Data Quality Audits as a Freelance Service (And What to Charge)

Most businesses have messy data. Few of them know the extent of the problem until it causes a visible failure. Even fewer have someone on staff whose job is to find and fix it.

That's the opportunity for a freelance data quality auditor.

Data quality auditing is a service you can offer as a standalone engagement, as an add-on to existing services, or as an entry point for larger data cleanup projects. The skill requirements are moderate, the competition is thin, and clients who understand the value are willing to pay well.

## What a Data Quality Audit Service Actually Is

A freelance data quality audit is a structured assessment of a client's dataset that produces a deliverable: a documented report showing what quality problems exist, how severe they are, and what to do about them.

The deliverable is the product. Unlike a cleanup engagement (where the deliverable is clean data), an audit engagement delivers analysis and recommendations. Some clients want just the audit. Others hire you for the audit first and the cleanup second. Both are legitimate scope structures.

A data quality audit includes:

- **Profiling**: Measuring completeness, uniqueness, validity, consistency, and timeliness across the dataset
- **Findings**: Documented list of quality failures with specific field names, metrics, and severity ratings
- **Root cause notes**: For significant findings, a hypothesis about what caused the problem
- **Recommendations**: Specific actions to address each finding, with an estimated complexity
- **Baseline documentation**: The quality metrics at the time of the audit, for before/after comparison

The audit scope can be as narrow as one spreadsheet or as broad as the client's entire data ecosystem. Define it clearly at engagement start.

## Who Needs This Service

**Small businesses before a CRM migration**: Moving data to a new system is high-risk. Companies often hire an auditor to assess what they have before the migration, so they know what needs to be cleaned first.

**E-commerce companies before peak season**: Before Black Friday or a major launch, an audit of customer and product data prevents campaign failures.

**Nonprofits before a major outreach campaign**: Donor and volunteer data often hasn't been maintained systematically. An audit before a fundraising campaign prevents bounce-heavy emails and wasted direct mail.

**Growing startups whose data has accumulated without governance**: A company that's been operating for 2–3 years often has significant data quality debt that needs to be assessed and addressed.

**Companies that just discovered a data problem**: A failed campaign, a wrong report, an import that broke — these create urgency and a clear need for an audit to understand what happened.

## How to Structure the Engagement

**Discovery call**: Understand the client's data — what system, what dataset, what it's used for, what specific concerns they have. This shapes the scope.

**Scope definition**: A written statement of what you'll audit (which dataset, which fields, which dimensions), what the deliverable looks like, and what's out of scope.

**Data access**: Client shares the data with you (typically a CSV export, shared spreadsheet, or read-only CRM access). You don't need edit access for an audit.

**Profiling and analysis**: Run the quality checks. Document what you find.

**Draft report**: Write up findings with specific numbers, severity ratings, and recommendations.

**Presentation call**: Walk the client through the report. Answer questions. Discuss priorities.

**Final report delivery**: Deliver the final documented report.

For a straightforward single-dataset audit, this is typically a 4–8 hour engagement start to finish, plus a 30-minute discovery call and a 30-minute presentation.

## What to Include in the Audit Report

**Executive summary** (1 page): Overall quality score, three most critical findings, recommended first action.

**Dataset overview**: What you audited — source, row count, field list, audit date.

**Findings table**: For each significant quality issue found:
- Field name
- Dimension (completeness, uniqueness, validity, consistency, timeliness)
- Metric (e.g., "email completeness: 87%")
- Severity (critical, high, medium, low)
- Business impact (what does this failure mean for the dataset's intended use?)
- Recommendation

**Root cause notes**: For critical and high findings, your analysis of why the problem exists.

**Remediation roadmap**: A prioritized list of recommended fixes with estimated effort.

**Appendix**: Raw profiling data — completeness rates per column, duplicate counts, validation failure counts.

## What to Charge

Pricing depends on your market, experience, and the complexity of the dataset. Starting points:

**Single-dataset audit (1 spreadsheet or export, up to 10,000 rows)**:
- Basic (completeness, duplicates, obvious validity): $250–$500
- Standard (full quality dimensions, findings table, recommendations): $500–$1,500
- Premium (full audit + root cause analysis + remediation roadmap + presentation): $1,500–$3,000

**Multi-dataset or CRM audit**:
- Multiple exports or a full CRM review: $2,000–$8,000+

**Retainer (ongoing quarterly audits)**:
- 1 dataset, quarterly: $300–$500/quarter
- Full data environment, quarterly: $1,500–$3,000/quarter

Don't underprice. Clients hiring for data quality audits understand they're buying professional expertise — pricing too low signals that you don't have it.

## How to Find Your First Clients

**Existing clients**: If you already do data work (VA, bookkeeping, marketing), offer a data quality audit as an add-on to your next engagement. "Before I start this project, let me run a quick audit on the data to identify any issues we should address first."

**Your network**: Ops managers and small business owners regularly complain about data problems on LinkedIn, in industry groups, and at events. Offer your service to people who are describing the problem.

**Niche communities**: Data quality audits for specific industries (healthcare, real estate, nonprofits) are particularly valuable. Position yourself in those communities.

**Content marketing**: Write about data quality for your target audience. A case study showing what you found in a client audit (anonymized) is a powerful lead generation piece.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need technical skills to offer data quality audits?**
Spreadsheet competence (Excel or Google Sheets formulas, pivot tables, filtering) covers the majority of small business audit work. Knowing how to use profiling tools handles larger files. Deep technical skills (Python, SQL) expand what you can audit but aren't required to start.

**Q: How do I handle NDA and confidentiality for client data?**
Use a standard freelance contract that includes data confidentiality provisions. For audits involving PII, add a data processing agreement (DPA) — standard under GDPR for any data processor in the EU. Many clients will ask for this proactively.

**Q: Can I use tools to speed up the audit?**
Yes — this improves your margin. Sohovi profiles your client's CSV in under a minute, producing completeness, uniqueness, and validity metrics per column that form the basis of your audit findings. This reduces profiling time from an hour to minutes, letting you spend your time on analysis and recommendations rather than manual counts.

**Q: How do I handle it when my findings reveal more problems than expected?**
Be honest with the client. Present the findings clearly and let them decide on scope. Many clients will expand the engagement when they see the scope of the problem. Some will take the report and handle cleanup internally.
